# If you were scammed: damage control

## First hour checklist
Money sent: call your bank or mobile-money provider immediately — some
transfers can be frozen if reported fast. Password entered on a fake site:
change it now everywhere you used it, starting with email, and enable
two-factor authentication. Remote access given: disconnect the device from
the internet and do not use it for banking until it is checked.

## Preserve the evidence
Do not delete the conversation. Screenshot messages, numbers, email addresses,
web addresses and payment references. Reports without evidence rarely go
anywhere; reports with screenshots and transaction IDs can.

## Do not pay to "recover" your money
Victims are routinely re-targeted by "recovery agents" who claim they can get
the money back for an upfront fee. This is the same scam wearing a new mask.
Only your bank, the payment platform, and the police are part of a real
recovery process.

## Tell someone
Scammers count on shame keeping victims silent. Telling family and reporting
the fraud is not embarrassing — staying silent is exactly what lets the same
scam reach the next person.
