# Recognizing phishing websites

## How to read a web address
The only part of a web address that matters is the registered domain — the part
just before .com, .org or similar. In "secure-paypal-login.example.tk", the
site is "example.tk", not PayPal. Scammers stuff trusted names into the front
of an address hoping you stop reading before the real domain.

## Signs of a fake login page
A page that asks for your password but lives on an address you have never seen,
uses a free domain ending like .tk or .xyz, shows subtle spelling errors, or
pressures you with warnings is very likely a phishing page. Real companies send
you to the same address every time — when in doubt, type the company's address
yourself instead of clicking a link.

## What to do about a suspicious link
Do not click it. If you already clicked, do not type anything. If you already
entered a password, change that password immediately everywhere you use it and
turn on two-factor authentication. If you entered card details, call your bank
using the number on the back of your card and ask them to block the card.
